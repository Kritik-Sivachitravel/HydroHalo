The Problem

Reusable bottles are
a hidden health risk

Narrow openings, rubber seals, built-in straws — modern bottle designs are nearly impossible to clean properly. Most people rinse and go. The result: bacterial counts that rival toilet seats, in the object people put their mouth on dozens of times a day.

What our customer discovery found

🎓
College students wash their bottles every few days at best — many cite lack of time and no dishwasher access in dorms.
🏃
Gym-goers and athletes refill multiple times daily but clean only when bottles smell or show visible buildup.
🏥
Healthcare workers carry bottles through patient areas all day. Full sanitization between patients simply isn't feasible during a busy shift.
✈️
Travelers refill bottles at water fountains across airports — high-traffic environments with no cleaning infrastructure.

How It Works

Three stages.
One clean bottle.

HydroHalo sequences each stage in the order the science recommends — rinse first to remove debris, dry to maximize UV-C efficiency, then sterilize.

💧
1
High-Pressure Rinse
A pressurized water spray flushes the bottle interior and lid — removing visible debris, residue, and early-stage biofilm before UV-C exposure. UV-C works by line-of-sight; a pre-rinse is the scientifically correct first step.
~20 seconds
💨
2
Warm-Air Drying
A blower fan circulates warm air through the bottle. Residual moisture scatters UV photons and reduces effective dose delivery. Drying before sterilization maximizes UV-C kill rate and prevents bacterial regrowth from moisture retention.
~10 seconds
✨
3
UV-C Sterilization
UV-C LEDs at 265–275 nm — the peak germicidal wavelength confirmed by DNA absorption research — irradiate the clean, dry interior. Photons are absorbed by bacterial DNA, forming pyrimidine dimers that permanently block replication.
~30 seconds
60s
Total cycle time
Place bottle → close door → retrieve a clean, dry, sterilized bottle
The Science

Not a trend.
100+ years of
proven science.

UV-C germicidal irradiation has been used in municipal water treatment since 1910. The mechanism is fully characterized at the molecular level: photons at 265 nm are absorbed by bacterial DNA, causing irreversible structural damage that permanently blocks cell division.

Adjacent thymine bases fuse (highlighted), forming cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs). DNA polymerase cannot copy across the lesion → cell cannot divide → bacterium is inactivated.
Key advantage
Bacteria cannot develop genetic resistance to UV-C. The physics of photon absorption do not change with microbial evolution — unlike antibiotics and chemical disinfectants.
Frontiers in Microbiology · 2021
Disinfection Performance of a Drinking Water Bottle System With a UV-C LED Cap Against Waterborne Pathogens
Mariita et al. · Crystal IS / Asahi Kasei · doi:10.3389/fmicb.2021.719578
The most directly applicable study to HydroHalo's concept: UV-C LED caps applied to inoculated stainless-steel water bottles (E. coli, P. aeruginosa, V. cholerae) under worst-case stationary conditions. 55-second UV-C cycle tested.
99.99% E. coli inactivation in 55 seconds (LRV4)

Scientific Reports (Nature) · 2026
UV-C LED Wavelength Effects on Inactivation Kinetics, DNA Damage and Membrane Integrity in Drinking Water Indicator Bacteria
Scientific Reports · doi:10.1038/s41598-026-44556-8
Tested five UV-C wavelengths (255, 260, 265, 270, 280 nm) against E. coli and Enterococcus faecium. 265 nm achieved highest inactivation efficiency per unit fluence. DNA lesions were largely irreversible — no significant bacterial recovery after exposure.
Up to 6-log inactivation below 7 mJ/cm² · 265nm confirmed optimal
→ Read full paper at Nature/Scientific Reports
Photochemical & Photobiological Sciences · 2023
Germicidal Efficacy of Continuous and Pulsed UV-C Radiation on Pathogen Models and SARS-CoV-2
Springer Nature · doi:10.1007/s43630-023-00521-2
Direct comparison of UV-C LEDs and mercury lamps across bacteria, endospores, and SARS-CoV-2. E. coli Log 4.1 reduction at 18 mJ/cm². SARS-CoV-2 Log 3.4+ reduction at 13.3 mJ/cm². No statistical difference between continuous and pulsed modes at equivalent dose.
Log 4.1 E. coli reduction · SARS-CoV-2 effectively inactivated

U.S. EPA / WHO · Regulatory Recognition
UV Disinfection Accepted for Municipal Drinking Water Treatment
EPA LT2ESWTR · WHO Drinking Water Guidelines
The U.S. EPA accepts UV disinfection as a method for drinking water plants to obtain inactivation credits for Cryptosporidium, Giardia, and viruses. WHO recognizes UV-C as an approved point-of-use water disinfection method. This regulatory acceptance reflects decades of demonstrated public health efficacy.

Market Opportunity

Four massive markets.
Zero direct competitors.

HydroHalo operates on a B2B placement model — targeting institutions where large numbers of people carry reusable bottles daily. No existing automated, brand-agnostic water bottle cleaning kiosk serves this institutional market.

✈️
Airports
~530
Commercial service airports in the U.S. (FAA, 2024). The FAA's Air Traffic Organization serves 3M+ passengers per day across 44,000+ daily flights. Post-security water refill stations are prime placement locations.
Source: FAA Air Traffic by the Numbers (FY2024) · BTS Top 50 U.S. Airports
🏥
Hospitals
6,100
Total U.S. hospitals as of 2024 (AHA 2024 Annual Survey). Infection control is a primary operational priority. Staff carry bottles into patient areas all day — a clear hygiene risk that facilities are motivated to address.
Source: American Hospital Association Fast Facts 2026 (2024 data)
🏋️
Gyms & Fitness Centers
77M
U.S. fitness facility members in 2024 — a record high, up 20% since 2019 (Health & Fitness Association). ~31,000 health clubs operate nationally. Members bring bottles to every class; sweat accelerates bacterial growth significantly.
Source: HFA 2025 U.S. Health & Fitness Consumer Report (April 2025)
🎓
Universities
5,819
Title IV postsecondary institutions in the 2023-24 academic year (NCES). Strong sustainability culture on campuses, existing water refill infrastructure, and a large captive user base. Penn State is our first pilot target.
Source: NCES IPEDS Press Release, August 21, 2024
